Henry VIII (1509–1547): Case Studies by Factor
Protestant Ideas
The Spread of Evangelical Ideas Before 1530

Pre-existing underground Protestant belief, independent of Henry's personal crisis, provides the strongest case that ideas, not politics, drove the Reformation from below. - By the 1530s, several thousand Lollards were practising their reformed religion in England, centred on Bible study in English; a hand-written copy of the reformed New Testament was each group's most prized possession - Contemporary observers by 1530 routinely called such people "evangelicals," not Lollards, indicating a live, spreading movement absorbed into the Lutheran moment - They fell into three overlapping groups: Cambridge academics; noble and near-noble families with Cambridge connections; and literate townspeople in London, Newcastle, Bristol, and other ports who had access to printed Lutheran literature - William Tyndale translated the New Testament into English, published in 1525 — treated by historians as one of the single most consequential events of the entire Reformation - Tyndale was a linguist of exceptional talent (fluent in seven languages), driven by the conviction that it was "impossible to establish lay-people in any truth" unless scripture was "plainly laid before their eyes in their mother tongue" - Blocked from publishing in England partly through Thomas More's opposition, he worked from exile in the Low Countries for eleven years - His translation became the textual basis of the state's own official Great Bible within little more than a decade, via Miles Coverdale's version — meaning underground Protestant labour became the foundation of official royal religious policy - Anne Boleyn was an evangelical sympathiser; she gave Henry a copy of Simon Fish's anticlerical pamphlet A Supplication for the Beggars (1528), which held Henry's attention for several months and framed clerical corruption in unmistakably Protestant terms

The Divorce Crisis, Anne Boleyn, and the Intellectual Content of the Break

The most historiographically nuanced case for Protestant ideas in Henry's reign: historians debate how genuine the evangelical conviction driving the break was, with Wooding's recent reassessment complicating any purely cynical reading. - Henry's initial response to Luther was the Assertio Septem Sacramentorum (1521), the first book written by an English king since Alfred the Great, written in Latin for a European audience and explicitly defending papal authority — Henry earned the title Fidei Defensor from a grateful Pope in reward - Thomas More, then Henry's counsellor, warned him against over-stressing the Pope's powers in the Assertio, since "his good relations with Rome might change" — a warning that proved prophetic - When Rome failed to deliver the annulment, Cranmer argued the divorce was a moral question on which the Bible supplied absolute answers, bypassing the Pope entirely and making Scripture the supreme authority - Anne Boleyn's own faith is characterised by Lucy Wooding (reviewing Guy and Fox's Hunting the Falcon) as "Lefèvrist" — after French reformer Jacques Lefèvre d'Étaples, whose Bible translations Anne's circle knew well; her brother George reportedly confessed on the scaffold, "I read the Gospel of Jesus Christ, but I did not follow it," not wishing to bring evangelicalism into disrepute - Wooding emphasises Henry's own faith was "deeply held, if idiosyncratic," and that his energetic personal participation in religious debate (detailed scrutiny of religious formulations, writing theological works himself) shows he took his role as Supreme Head with genuine seriousness, not merely as a political convenience - Henry and Anne regularly discussed theology at dinner and shared evangelical texts, making the break partly the product of shared intellectual conviction rather than purely personal and dynastic necessity - However, Wooding also stresses Anne remained doctrinally orthodox in practice — she attended Mass, gave alms, defended the monasteries, and sought confession and absolution in the Tower before her execution — making the Protestant/Catholic binary too clean a frame for understanding her evangelicalism
